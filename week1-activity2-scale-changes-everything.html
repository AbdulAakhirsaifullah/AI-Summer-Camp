📈 Scale Changes Everything

Track B · Week 1 · Activity 2 — Why Quick Draw is so much better than your model

15 min

Play a few rounds first

Open Quick Draw and play at least 3 rounds. You'll get 20 seconds per drawing — AI tries to guess what you're sketching in real time, updating its guess with every stroke.

🔗 Open Quick Draw

The numbers behind it

In Activity 1, you trained your gesture classifier on your own data. Quick Draw's model was trained very differently. Compare the scale:

Your classifier
~120
images total (≈40 × 3 gestures), from 1 person, 1 room, 1 lighting setup
Quick Draw
50,000,000
drawings, from 15 million people, across the entire world
That bar isn't a rendering bug — your dataset really is that close to zero on this scale. Quick Draw's is roughly 400,000× larger.

Discussion — answer as you play

1. Why does it recognize some drawings instantly and struggle with others?

2. How is 50 million drawings from 15 million people different from your 40 webcam photos in Activity 1?

3. What happens when you draw something in an unusual style — does it still get it?

4. Watch the guesses change stroke by stroke. What does that tell you about how the model processes your input in real time?

The key contrast

Your Teachable Machine model is narrow and fragile — it had ~120 examples from one person. Quick Draw is robust and fast because it had 50 million. Scale of training data changes everything — and this idea sits behind modern AI like ChatGPT, which is trained on a scale even larger than Quick Draw's.
